Problem

OVO had millions of active users. Superbank had zero.

Converting one into the other meant bridging two very different user expectations: the instant simplicity of an e-wallet and the regulatory weight of a licensed bank. That gap was our design problem.

First, intent. Most OVO users already had a primary bank. We weren't competing with other banks. We were competing with "I don't need this."

Second, friction. Bank registration is heavy by nature — KTP, personal data, 21 regulatory fields. Inside an app built for instant payments, this felt jarring. Users who had already decided to upgrade were dropping off not because they changed their mind, but because the experience stopped them.

Third, continuity. Onboarding was just the beginning. Payments and transaction history had to feel consistent across two separate apps with different systems and design languages. A disjointed post-onboarding experience would undo all the trust we built getting users there.

"How might we design a banking upgrade inside OVO that feels like a natural next step — not a whole new product?"

Challenges

Complex Organization. Superbank, OVO, and Grab operated under different structures, priorities, and decision-making models, making cross-company alignment challenging.

Fragmented Product Surfaces. App flows, accounts, and wallets lived in different paradigms, creating risks of dual states, unclear ownership, and user confusion — even branding was still evolving.

Tight Timeline & Regulatory Constraints. We had to validate the MVP quickly while building a scalable foundation that remained fully compliant with regulatory requirements.

Approach

Defining CVP as the Conversion Engine

Converting passive OVO users into active Superbank customers came down to two levers working together.

The first was economic. Unlike a typical e-wallet, OVO Nabung lets users earn up to 5% interest on their idle balance — turning money that was just sitting there into something actively working for them. A concrete, immediate reason to upgrade.

The second was psychological. The biggest barrier in digital banking isn't the form — it's the perception of starting over. So we didn't frame it that way. We positioned the entire experience as an upgrade to something users already had — staying inside OVO's familiar environment, with existing data pre-filled, and Superbank introduced quietly in the background.

Branding

Co-Branding Strategy

A co-branding strategy was implemented between OVO and Superbank with a clear principle: users should never feel like they are moving to a different platform.

OVO remains the dominant visual and brand presence. Superbank is introduced in a subtle, supportive role. The overall experience is designed to feel like an evolution of OVO — not a migration.

Solution

Minimizing Friction Without Compromising Compliance

The compliance team required all 21 fields upfront. Splitting the flow meant users would exist in a partially-verified state — a regulatory concern.

I mapped each field to the product it actually served: some were required for savings (identity, address), others specifically for lending (income, source of funds, risk profiling). This gave us the legal basis for the split.

The result: permissive for savings, strict for lending.

Design

Flow 1: Lite Upgrade (OVO App)

The first flow lives entirely within the OVO app. By leveraging OVO's existing KYC data, I reduced the registration form from 21 fields down to 3 — only collecting what Superbank needed that OVO didn't already have.

This was the critical moment: after completing just 3 fields, the user has an active OVO Nabung account.

Integration

Integration Across OVO Journey

Instead of introducing the upgrade as a separate initiative, I strategically embedded conversion levers across the existing OVO onboarding journey and key touchpoints.

The upgrade should feel like a natural progression, not a handoff to another product. By integrating awareness, value cues, and upgrade triggers directly into familiar moments, the transition felt seamless — more like an evolution within OVO than a distinct flow.

Reflection

Given the tight timeline, I made a deliberate decision to prioritize launch speed over perfecting the messaging. This meant making strategic trade-offs between clearly communicating the CVP and simplifying the onboarding flow.

Post-MVP launch, I closely analyzed user feedback and behavioral data. The insights were clear: users struggled to understand the interest rate, expressed concerns about hidden bank fees, and showed early signs of trust friction.

During launch, this acquisition initiative helped Superbank reach its first 1 million users in under one year — a significant milestone for a new bank in Indonesia. As of 2026, 60% of Superbank users originate from the OVO Nabung journey.
